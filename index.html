Hidden within forest canopies and sun-warmed timber, the jewel beetle is one of nature's most spectacular optical illusions — a living gemstone that has captivated humans, artists, and scientists for millennia.

01 — Habitat

Where They Thrive

Jewel beetles colonise every continent except Antarctica, favouring warm, humid forests where dead or dying wood is abundant. Many species are critically dependent on old-growth trees, making them key indicators of forest ecosystem health. Larvae spend years tunnelling through heartwood, reshaping the forest from within.

02 — Diet

Timber & Pollen

Adult jewel beetles feed on nectar, pollen, and foliage, becoming accidental pollinators as they move between flowers. Their larvae, however, are dedicated xylophages — burrowing into phloem and heartwood where they digest cellulose-rich material using specialised gut microbiota, a biological feat still under scientific investigation.

03 — Behaviour

Pyrophilous Senses

Certain jewel beetles (Melanophila) possess infrared pit organs on their thorax — among the most sensitive heat detectors in the animal kingdom. They detect forest fires from 80 km away and race toward smouldering trees to lay eggs in freshly charred wood, before competitors arrive. Fire is their opportunity.

Structural Colour · Photonics

Not Pigment,
But Physics

The jewel beetle's extraordinary shimmer contains no coloured dye. Its elytra are layered with thin-film nanostructures — stacked cuticle lamellae just 70–200 nm thick. Incident light reflects from each layer, interfering constructively at specific wavelengths to produce colours that shift and sing as the viewing angle changes. Scientists have replicated this structure to create next-generation anti-counterfeiting materials and bio-inspired optical films.

Life Cycle

Stage 01

Egg

Females lay eggs in bark crevices or freshly scorched wood. Each clutch may contain 40–100 eggs. Hatching occurs within 1–3 weeks depending on temperature.

Stage 02

Larva

The legless larva bores sinuous galleries deep into wood, feeding for 1–5 years. This phase sculpts the distinctive winding trails visible in salvaged timber.

Stage 03

Pupa

The larva carves a pupal chamber near the surface and metamorphoses for 3–6 weeks as the jewelled shell and compound eyes take form beneath old skin.

Stage 04

Adult

The adult chews a perfectly oval exit hole and emerges to live just 2–6 weeks — its sole purpose to feed, find a mate, and ensure the next generation.
